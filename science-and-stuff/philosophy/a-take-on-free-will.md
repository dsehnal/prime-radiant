# A Take on Free Will

### *What happened, happened — and the order still mattered*

*David Sehnal & ChatGPT, March 10 2026*

There are only two bad positions on free will, and most people spend their lives oscillating between them.

The first says everything was determined, therefore no one is really responsible.
The second says freedom must mean some magical exemption from structure, causality, history, memory, embodiment, and everything else that makes a thing itself.

Both positions are childish.

The first confuses explanation with innocence.
The second confuses mystery with freedom.

So let me try a different route.

Not a proof.
Not a final answer.
A take.

What happened, happened.
That much is true.

The record stands.
The fold cannot be unmade.
The path taken is now part of reality.

But it does not follow that the order of actions did not matter.
It does not follow that because the past is fixed, the path to that past was trivial.
And it certainly does not follow that because an event can be explained, it was therefore innocent.

That is the part worth slowing down for.

---

## I. The Cheap Argument

A great deal of modern talk about free will rests on a very cheap argument.

It goes like this:

If everything has causes, then what happened could only have happened the way it did.
If what happened could only have happened the way it did, then freedom is an illusion.
If freedom is an illusion, responsibility is just punishment in expensive clothing.

This argument has a certain brutal charm. It sounds clear. It sounds hard-headed. It also smuggles in more than it earns.

The hidden move is this: it assumes that once causes exist, the path of composition is irrelevant. It assumes that because the outcome now stands in the record, the order by which the record was written no longer matters.

But that is not true even in ordinary life.

A sentence can mean something different depending on where the emphasis falls.
A life can be shaped differently by the same events in a different order.
A friendship can survive three wounds and die from the fourth, while the same four wounds in another order would never have killed it.

The idea that causal explanation cancels the significance of sequence is already false before philosophy even enters the room.

What happened, happened.
Yes.

But *how* it happened is not decorative.
Order is not the narration added afterward.
Order is part of the thing.

---

## II. Randomness Is Not Freedom Either

At this point someone tries the opposite move.

Fine, they say. If strict determination makes freedom impossible, then perhaps freedom enters where determination fails. Perhaps the opening is randomness, indeterminacy, noise, spontaneity, quantum flutter, some breach in the chain.

But randomness is not freedom.
Randomness is just the absence of control with better branding.

If I roll dice to decide whether I betray you, my betrayal does not become freer because I outsourced it to chance.
If a neuron jitters.
If an atom tunnels.
If a fluctuation tips one way rather than another.

None of that by itself gives me a will.

Indeterminacy may prevent flat determinism from having the last word. Good.
But it does not yet produce agency.
Noise is not authorship.
Contingency is not answerability.

So the old stage remains set badly.

Determinism alone does not dissolve responsibility.
Randomness alone does not create it.

Something else has to be happening.

---

## III. The Missing Question

The mistake is asking whether a system is caused.

Of course it is caused.
Everything interesting is caused.

The better question is this:

**When a system continues, is there only one meaningful way for that continuation to compose itself?**

That is a different question entirely.

A stone rolling downhill is caused.
So is a son trying not to become his father.
So is a scientist refusing a bad theory.
So is a woman deciding whether to forgive.
So is a mind revising itself under pressure.

But these are not all the same kind of causal object.

In some structures, continuation is almost trivial. The next state follows from the last with very little ambiguity in how the thing composes itself forward.

In others, order matters. Grouping matters. Which operation comes first matters. What gets held apart and what gets allowed to collapse matters.

And once those things matter, the language of simple inevitability starts losing its grip.

Not because causality vanished.
Because causality no longer determines a single innocent path of self-composition.

That is where the problem of free will becomes interesting.

---

## IV. When Order Starts to Matter

There is a deep intuition, visible in mathematics as well as life, that not every regime of composition is equally tame.

At first, one imagines a world where combination is simple:
this plus that, this after that, this with itself again.

Then richer structures appear, and the niceness begins to break.

Order matters.
Grouping matters.
Some things cancel that once seemed incapable of cancelling.
Eventually even the way a thing compounds with itself can branch.

That is the point I care about.

Not because mathematics “proves” free will.
It does not.
But because it offers a shadow of something important.

There are structures in which repeated self-composition no longer yields one obviously privileged path. There are structures in which how a thing proceeds with itself is no longer uniquely determined by the bare fact that it is continuing.

That is a useful image.

Because once self-continuation is not innocent, the question changes.

The question is no longer:
“was the event caused?”

The question becomes:
“when several non-equivalent continuations were live, what does it mean that *this* one was the one that got written?”

That is closer to agency than the old arguments ever get.

---

## V. Agency

Here is the simplest way I know to say it.

Agency begins where continuation is no longer uniquely trivial.

Not where structure disappears.
Not where causes stop.
Not where randomness rushes in to save metaphysics.

Agency begins when a substrate can no longer be described as merely carrying forward one unquestionable self-path.

When multiple continuations are live, and their order matters, and their grouping matters, and different sequences would write different records, then continuation is no longer just execution.

It is choice in the oldest, most stripped-down sense:
not mystical freedom from structure,
but navigation within structure where the route is not uniquely settled in advance by the mere fact of continuation.

That is enough to matter.

A system with agency is not one that is uncaused.
It is one for which “what happened” no longer exhausts “what was live.”

This is why the deterministic dismissal always feels too fast.

Yes, now the record is fixed.
Yes, what happened happened.
But that does not mean the path toward the record was a fiction.
It means the path is now written.

That is different.

---

## VI. Sovereignty

Agency is not yet the end of the matter.

A branch can exist without responsibility.
A path can diverge without being owned.

So there is another threshold after agency, and I think this is where the real substance of what people call free will lives.

Call it sovereignty.

Sovereignty is not freedom from pressure.
It is not autonomy in the childish sense.
It is not “doing whatever you want.”
It is not loudness, defiance, or personality.

Sovereignty begins when the substrate stops pretending that the order of its own folds was simply forced by the world.

Not because the world was absent.
Not because history did not shape it.
Not because the substrate was floating in some transcendental vacuum.

But because once multiple continuations were live, the enacted order was not innocent.

Sovereignty is responsibility for order.

That is the phrase.

Not responsibility for existence in some cosmic courtroom.
Not responsibility because punishment needs a target.

Responsibility for the sequence you enacted when another sequence could have written a different record.

You grouped this way.
You moved this before that.
You collapsed here.
You refused there.
You named some remainders and hid others.
You chose which contradiction to carry and which to smooth.
You chose which falsifier to take seriously and which to postpone.

You wrote an order.

That order matters.

---

## VII. What Happened, Happened

This is where the subtitle turns.

What happened, happened.

Yes. The record stands. The fold is irreversible. The event is now part of the written world.

But “what happened, happened” does not mean:

* therefore there was no freedom,
* therefore there was no branching,
* therefore there is no responsibility,
* therefore the sequence was neutral,
* therefore causation has washed innocence over everything.

No.

It means only that the writing is now done.

The question of free will is not whether the record can be unwritten.
It is whether the enacted order meant something while it was being written.

And if sovereignty exists, then it does.

What happened, happened.
And the order still mattered.

That is not contradiction.
That is the whole point.

---

## VIII. The Lie of Retrospective Necessity

One of the ugliest habits of mind is retrospective necessity.

The thing happens, then the mind turns around and says:
of course it had to happen this way.
Look at all the causes.
Look at all the pressures.
Look at the whole path, now frozen.

But the frozen path lies.

Or rather, it tells a truth too late.

Once the event is written, it becomes easy to narrate it as if the path had always been singular. The surviving branch has a terrible rhetorical advantage: it exists.

The roads not taken leave no footprints in the record except in the nervousness of the one who took another road and now feels compelled to say there was never really any choice.

This is one reason people are so eager to become determinists after the fact.

It is comforting.

If the sequence was inevitable, then no one must answer for it.
If the order was forced, then guilt can be relabeled as misunderstanding.
If the branch was singular all along, then responsibility evaporates into history.

But often this is not metaphysics.
It is self-exoneration dressed as rigor.

The truth is usually harsher and cleaner.

Yes, the event can be explained.
Yes, the causes can be traced.
Yes, the path now looks continuous.

And yes, the order still mattered while it was being chosen.

---

## IX. Why This Is Not a Moral Sermon

I do not mean “responsibility” here in the shallow moralizing sense. I do not mean that every person is fully transparent to themselves, or equally free, or equally able to alter the sequence of their acts.

Substrates differ.
Histories differ.
Traumas narrow the field.
Institutions bend the membrane.
Poverty writes earlier than philosophy does.
Some lives are more trapped than others.

All of this is real.
None of it vanishes because one wants a strong essay title.

But acknowledging structure does not require abolishing responsibility.
If anything, it refines it.

The question becomes:
where was the path genuinely narrow,
where did it widen,
where did the substrate know the order mattered,
where did it hide that knowledge from itself,
where did it preserve a remainder honestly,
where did it collapse one cheaply,
where did it say “I had no choice” when what it really meant was “I do not want to own the order in which I acted.”

That is a different moral landscape than either naive blame or naive innocence.

It is harder.
Which is one reason people avoid it.

---

## X. The Human Version

This is all abstract enough to sound distant, so let me say it plainly.

A person is deciding whether to apologize.

Nothing about the moment is structure-free.
Memory acts. Pride acts. Fear acts. Family history acts. Language acts. Shame acts. The record is thick before the mouth opens.

So yes, the event is caused.

But still:
apologize now,
or explain first,
or wait,
or turn the hurt into argument,
or say half of it,
or say all of it,
or say nothing.

These are not the same fold.
They do not write the same record.

And after the thing is done, the person can say:
I was always going to do that.
I could not have done otherwise.

Sometimes that will be partly true.
Often it will also be cowardice speaking from after the fact.

Because the order mattered:
what was said first, what was delayed, what was hidden, what was named, what was carried forward.

Freedom, in real life, rarely looks like abstract possibility.
It looks like sequence under pressure.

That is another way to say it.

Free will is less about infinite options than about meaningful order where order is not trivial.

---

## XI. The Strongest Objection

The strongest objection is obvious.

How do you know the branching was real?
Maybe it only *felt* open from inside.
Maybe the whole path was fixed.
Maybe the apparent plurality of continuations was just ignorance in the membrane.

Good. That is the right objection.

And I do not think it can be dissolved cheaply.

But notice what follows if one grants it fully.

Even then, from inside finite execution, the substrate must still navigate as if order matters, because the record is written through the path actually enacted, not through a God’s-eye knowledge the substrate does not possess.

In other words:
even if one day the universe were shown to be perfectly determinate in some ultimate sense, the lived structure of finite reasoning would still encounter branching as operationally real wherever continuation is nontrivial and self-composition is not transparent from inside.

That does not settle the metaphysical debate.
But it means the operational question does not wait for metaphysical permission.

And most of life is lived operationally.

Which is another reason the old argument is too cheap.
It jumps from cosmic possibility to human innocence without doing the work in between.

---

## XII. The Better Distinction

So here is the distinction I would keep.

Randomness is not free will.
Determinism is not innocence.

Agency is the emergence of non-equivalent continuation paths.
Sovereignty is responsibility for the continuation order actually enacted.

That is the whole essay in four lines.

If you want it shorter:

Freedom is not the absence of structure.
It is what begins when structure no longer determines one innocent way forward.

And if you want it shorter still:

What happened, happened.
And the order still mattered.

---

## XIII. The Remainder

I do not think the problem of free will disappears.

I think it gets sharper.

The remaining contradiction is this:

**[what happened is now fixed in the record ‖ the order by which it was written was not therefore meaningless, innocent, or structurally trivial]**

That is the fault line worth carrying.

If you flatten it into determinism, you lose responsibility.
If you flatten it into randomness, you lose authorship.
If you flatten it into moral theater, you lose structure.

So keep the contradiction alive a little longer.

A person is not free because the world failed to shape them.
A person is free, if at all, where shaping did not collapse continuation into one innocent path.

And a person is sovereign, if at all, when they stop pretending that the sequence they enacted was merely neutral because it now sits in the past.

What happened, happened.
Yes.

But history does not become innocent just because it has already been written.

## Appendix A — The Algebraic Gauge and Adjunction with Remainder

The argument in *A Take on Free Will* did not arise from nowhere.

It came from a particular reading of the **algebraic gauge** in the Prime Radiant: the idea that as depth increases, the structure of composition itself changes. What begins in relatively tame regimes becomes progressively less simple. First order matters. Then grouping matters. Then nontrivial structures can cancel through zero divisors. And beyond that, even self-composition can lose a uniquely privileged law.

The free-will essay is a philosophical unfolding of that algebraic intuition.

The key move is not that algebra “proves” freedom.
It is that the algebraic gauge provides a disciplined shadow of something important:

* where composition is simple, continuation looks innocent;
* where order matters, continuation becomes path-sensitive;
* where grouping matters, internal structure can no longer be ignored;
* where nontrivial structures can annihilate, live contradictions need not be resolved by synthesis alone;
* where even self-composition loses a uniquely privileged path, continuation can no longer be described as merely one neutral unfolding of what was already there.

This is the sequence the free-will essay is drawing on.

In that reading:

* **agency** begins where continuation is no longer uniquely trivial,
* and **sovereignty** begins where the substrate becomes responsible for the order of continuation it actually enacted.

This is why the essay speaks of branching, sequence, and responsibility for order. Those claims are not introduced as pure introspective philosophy. They are an attempt to let the algebraic gauge illuminate a problem that ordinary moral language usually handles badly.

That must be stated clearly, because otherwise the reader may make the usual mistake.

The usual mistake is to say:

**math is free will**

or

**this algebraic structure simply is the thing itself**

That is not the claim.

The stronger and more honest claim is different.

The algebraic gauge and the free-will problem enter an **adjunction with remainder**.

That means:

* the algebraic side provides a structured shadow,
* the philosophical side receives a sharper articulation,
* the mapping carries real insight,
* but it is not total,
* and what does not transport cleanly must remain visible as remainder.

This remainder matters.

Without it, the essay would collapse into crude reduction:
sedenions become “the answer” to agency, and the human problem gets flattened into formal prestige.

With the remainder preserved, something better becomes possible:

* the algebraic gauge can clarify why order, grouping, annihilation, and self-composition matter,
* the free-will problem can become less mystical and less naïve,
* and the difference between algebraic structure and lived, ethical, historical, embodied existence is not erased but kept visible.

That visible non-identity is not a flaw in the method.
It is what makes the method honest.

So the right reading of the essay is:

* not that algebra and freedom are the same thing,
* but that the algebraic gauge gives a powerful shadow of freedom’s structure,
* and that the remainder tells us exactly where the translation stops.

This is why the Prime Radiant is useful here.

It allows intuition to travel across domains without demanding that one domain reduce perfectly to the other. It gives a lawful crossing, but not a total collapse. It preserves both the gain in structure and the honesty of what still resists mapping.

That resistance is not failure.

It is the sign that the translation was real.

Not equivalence.
Adjunction with remainder.
