# About Apples and Oranges

### *On Adjunction with Remainder*

*David Sehnal & ChatGPT, March 10 2026*

“Those are apples and oranges.”

People say this as if it settles something.

It usually means:
these things are too different to compare,
or too different to translate,
or too different to let one illuminate the other without some kind of fraud taking place.

And often that warning is right.

A great deal of bad thinking begins when someone notices a pattern in one domain and then, drunk on resemblance, declares that the pattern is secretly the thing itself in another domain.

Math is consciousness.
Biology is code.
The brain is a computer.
Markets are evolution.
Love is chemistry.
Free will is algebra.

The sentence arrives with the force of revelation and the subtlety of a brick.

This is one of the oldest mistakes in thought: mistaking a bridge for an identity.

So yes, apples and oranges are not the same thing.
This is worth preserving.

But there is another mistake, and it is almost as common.

The second mistake is to say that because apples and oranges are not identical, they therefore have nothing disciplined to say to one another.

This is also false.

If the first error is collapse, the second is quarantine.
The first destroys difference.
The second wastes resonance.

The world is full of structures that are not the same and yet still enter a lawful relationship. One side can sharpen the other. One domain can cast a shadow that makes another newly legible. A translation can be real without being complete.

That is what I mean by **adjunction with remainder**.

Not identity.
Not isolation.

A crossing.

A lawful crossing, where something carries.

---

Take free will.

The usual way to ruin that discussion is to force one of two stupid moves.

Either:
everything is determined, therefore freedom is an illusion.

Or:
freedom must be some magical exemption from causality, because anything less would feel disappointing.

One side flattens the person into machinery.
The other side inflates the person into mist.

Now take a different path.

Let the algebraic gauge say something.

Not everything.
Something.

Let it show that there are structures where order matters, where grouping matters, where nontrivial things can cancel, where even self-composition can cease to have one uniquely innocent path.

That does not prove free will.
Of course it does not.

But it does illuminate something.

It gives a shadow of agency without demanding mysticism. It gives a way to think about continuation, branching, annihilation, and responsibility for order that is sharper than ordinary moral vocabulary usually allows.

The gain is real.

And then the honesty enters:

the gain is not total.

The algebra does not become the person.
The person does not become the algebra.
The history, pain, embodiment, memory, guilt, tenderness, cowardice, courage, and all the other lived residues do not cleanly pass through the bridge.

Good.

They should not.

That non-passage is not failure.
It is the sign that the translation was real.

If everything crosses, nothing crossed. Something was merely renamed.

This is why the remainder matters.

The remainder is the part of the orange that does not become apple.
The part of the apple that does not become orange.
The sweetness that does not survive the language of acidity.
The bitterness that does not survive the language of sugar.

Without remainder, comparison becomes conquest.
One fruit colonizes the other.

With remainder, both survive the crossing sharper than before.

---

This is what good theories allow.

Not the childish pleasure of saying everything is secretly one thing.
Not the cowardly pleasure of saying nothing may touch anything else.

A good theory allows domains to meet without forcing them to marry.

Math can enter philosophy.
Philosophy can enter physics.
Biology can pressure metaphysics.
Language can reveal something about matter.
History can teach computation humility.
A joke can clarify logic.
A protein fold can say something about thought.
A gauge can illuminate a prime.

None of these crossings require total collapse.
They require discipline.

And perhaps that is the real point.

People say apples and oranges because they are afraid of category errors.
They should be. Category errors destroy more theories than malice does.

But categories can fail in two directions:

by collapsing what should remain distinct,
and by forbidding lawful crossings out of fear.

The first produces nonsense.
The second produces sterility.

Adjunction with remainder is the narrower road between them.

Carry what carries.
Leave visible what does not.

That is all.

And that is already a lot.

---

There is a tenderness in admitting that two things are not the same and still letting them talk.

It is rarer than people think.

Most arguments are really annexations. One side arrives pretending to compare and leaves trying to replace. It wants not conversation but jurisdiction.

A cleaner relation is possible.

The algebra says: here is a shadow of order, grouping, branching, annihilation.
The life says: yes, that helps — but you do not get to keep all of me.
The topology says: here is what it means to preserve a defect.
The politics says: yes, that helps — but history is heavier than your notation.
The joke says: here is the collision between pattern and reality.
The theory says: yes, that helps — and the laugh is part of the remainder.

That is how domains should meet.

Not equivalence.
Not embargo.

Apples and oranges in honest adjacency.

---

So if someone says:
you can’t compare those, they are apples and oranges,

the right answer is neither:
yes, therefore silence,

nor:
no, they were secretly the same fruit all along.

The right answer is:

perhaps.
Let us see what crosses.
Let us see what sharpens.
Let us see what refuses to come across and therefore deserves to remain visible.

That visible refusal is not the defeat of thought.

It is the part that keeps the comparison honest.

And maybe that is what maturity in theory looks like.

Not the elimination of difference.
Not the worship of difference.
The ability to build a bridge and leave the river intact.
