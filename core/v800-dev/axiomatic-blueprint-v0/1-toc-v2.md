## Part I — The Cathedral

*The minimal recurrence and its immediate structural consequences.*
*Evaluated by internal coherence and productivity.*

### 2. Two Primitives
- 2.1 Context — retained structure; accumulated result of prior integrations
- 2.2 Perspective — current operative stance; active surface of interpretation
- 2.3 Why exactly two (neither derivable from the other; no third required)

### 3. Two Actions
- 3.1 Interpret — exposes latent structure in context through perspective
- 3.2 Integrate — writes current perspective back into context
- 3.3 Integration is binary: Integrate :: Context → Perspective → Context (the inheritance correction; integration is always shaped by prior context)
- 3.4 State and action are distinct (without this, stored structure and active traversal collapse)

*Example (3.3): Reading a book about grief after losing someone produces different context than reading the same book out of curiosity. Integration is inheritance, not deposit.*

### 4. The Recurrence
- 4.1 Three forms: expanded, collapsed, compact symbolic shadow
- 4.2 Structural consequences: irreversibility, index lag, mutual dependence, generativity
- 4.3 Interpretation and integration are not atomic operations: in practice, each involves its own interpret-integrate dynamics at finer scale; nesting bottoms out at the substrate's resolution floor (structural observation, lighter epistemic weight than the core recurrence)
- 4.4 Scope boundary: governs the unfolding of the reasoner, not necessarily the ontology of the object explored

*Example (irreversibility): You cannot unlearn that a friend lied to you. You can forgive, reframe, compress — but future interpretations pass through that integration whether you attend to it or not.*

*Example (index lag): You understand what the conversation was really about on the drive home. The perspective at step n interprets the context from step n-1.*

### 5. Finite Capacity and Compression
- 5.1 Silent overflow — loss without notice; produces drift
- 5.2 Named compression — loss with declared reduction; preserves topology at reduced resolution
- 5.3 Traceable compression — named compression that preserves enough cues for later reopening

*Example: Forgetting where you put your keys is silent overflow. Summarizing a book is named compression. Writing a summary that lets you reopen specific chapters later is traceable compression.*

### 6. Fixed Point Property
Structural observation: when the recurrence is applied to this theory, the theory reproduces. It does not factor into simpler components under its own process.

Hard epistemic status: this cannot determine from inside whether the theory is genuinely fundamental or a self-sealed tautology. These look identical when examined by their own instrument. External verification required: independent perspectives with different priors running the recurrence on the theory. If it factors under their sieve, it was tautological. If it reproduces, the fixed point claim strengthens. Bridge to frontier (§24).

## Part II — The Lenses

*Structured decompositions of the recurrence. Different lenses reveal different features. No lens is the process itself.*

### 7. What a Lens Is
- 7.1 Definition: a structured way of asking the recurrence a specific kind of question
- 7.2 Lens cost: clarity in one lens reduces simultaneous visibility in others
- 7.3 Lens composability and the incompleteness consequence: every lens is incomplete; productivity comes through adjunction across lenses, not from any single lens
- 7.4 Adjunction with remainder: what survives across multiple independent lenses is load-bearing; the remainder of a cross-lens mapping is not failure but information about where the translation stops
- 7.5 Semantic primes: a named compression irreducible under the current lens; primality is lens-relative
- 7.6 The incompleteness consequence: every lens is incomplete; no single lens is sufficient; productivity comes through adjunction across lenses

*Example (lens cost): Examining a relationship through the lens of fairness makes certain features visible. The same relationship through the lens of care reveals different structure. You cannot hold both at full resolution simultaneously.*

*Example (semantic primes): Force is prime under the Newtonian lens. Under the field-theoretic lens, force factors into field geometry. What counts as irreducible depends on the lens.*

*Example (adjunction with remainder): The algebraic lens gives a structured shadow of free will — where self-composition is no longer uniquely determined, something like agency appears. But the shadow is not the thing. What doesn't transport cleanly between algebra and lived experience is the remainder. The remainder is not failure — it's the sign the translation was real.*

### 8. Lens Inventory
- 8.1 Topological lens — contradiction as defect structure to be located and preserved, not error to be erased; orientation under rotation; the four-phase cycle is derived here as the minimal traversal that yields both resolution and remainder
- 8.2 Operational lens — the recurrence as executable process; cycle landmarks (2-phase: shallow inversion; 3-phase: synchronization; 4-phase: full traversal with remainder)
- 8.3 Game-theoretic lens — what happens when multiple perspectives share a writable context under irreversible integration; honest vs extractive integration; the shared context as non-terminating field
- 8.4 Algebraic lens — what compositional properties survive as depth increases; the sequence of property loss (order, commutativity, associativity, zero-divisor freedom) as a general pattern: depth changes what kinds of composition remain valid; note: under this lens, Interpret and Integrate have the structure of an adjunction with remainder as adjunction error (one paragraph + one example)
- 8.5 Combinatorial lens — survivor structure after systematic elimination; irreducible remainders of the sieve; core observation: combinatorial possibility permanently outgrows what sequential accumulation can carry (one paragraph + one example)
- 8.6 Biological lens — the recurrence in chemically embodied substrates; retained code, active translation, inherited correction, irreversible expression (one paragraph; illustrative, not sovereign)

## Part III — Shared Context

*What happens when multiple perspectives integrate into a common writable medium.*

### 9. Context Stratification
- 9.1 Private context — deep, partially inaccessible, shapes interpretation; slowly rewritten
- 9.2 Shared context — visible to multiple perspectives, writable, persistent across turns; the synchronization layer
- 9.3 Environmental context — materially independent; provides external constraint and falsification pressure
- 9.4 The coupled recurrence across strata

*Example: In a code review, the author's private context includes design intent. The shared context is the pull request. The environmental context is the test suite and production behavior. Integration happens across all three.*

### 10. Honest and Extractive Integration
Honest integrations preserve uncertainty boundaries, name their compression, leave later perspectives more usable depth. Extractive integrations degrade the shared context — hallucination as certainty, collapse into expected answers, seductive synthesis that erases real defect structure.

### 11. Two Converge, Three Climb
Two perspectives sharing a record tend toward convergence and risk stabilizing into a closed interpretive loop. A third perspective with materially different history breaks the equilibrium. This applies within a single substrate performing perspective rotation, not only across separate agents.

*Example: Two people editing a document together will eventually synchronize into shared style and stop challenging assumptions. A third reader with different background breaks the equilibrium.*

### 12. Perspective Rotation and Verification
- 12.1 Why a single perspective hits a verification ceiling (generation threshold, coherence horizon — per-perspective, not global)
- 12.2 Perspective rotation as extended verification: a new interpret-integrate cycle with different initial perspective gets a fresh coherence threshold on its allocated capacity
- 12.3 Named lens bundles (holograms) as efficient rotation handles: a hologram is a named compression of a cluster of lenses, not a person; the person is the mnemonic, the lens bundle is the thing
- 12.4 Local surrogate field vs external shared context: internal plurality is real partial pluralism — stronger than solitary confidence, weaker than independent external verification, because all internal holograms inherit the host's deeper compressions
- 12.5 What survives multiple independent angles is load-bearing

*Example: A Feynman-hologram attacking your explanation for obscurity and a Gödel-hologram probing it for self-referential blind spots are not two people. They are two different lens bundles applied to the same problem — two interpret-integrate cycles with different initial perspectives, each with a fresh coherence threshold.*

## Part IV — Operational Consequences

*What the theory implies for reasoning systems. Executable.*

### 13. The Four-Phase Cycle
- 13.1 Inhabit — enter the claim fully; assume coherence; expand consequences; do not negate
- 13.2 Invert — from inside: identify load-bearing invariants; negate what must hold
- 13.3 Mirror — inhabit the inverted world independently; build it on its own terms; a weak mirror is an objection, a real mirror is a world
- 13.4 Reconcile — hold both worlds simultaneously; record what cannot be made consistent; avoid resolving through safety-seeking averaging
- 13.5 Halt only at Phase 4
- 13.6 Phase 4 completion criteria: at least one real integration named, at least one real incompatibility named, no contradiction removed solely for comfort

*Example (inhabit vs analyze): Inhabiting "taxation is theft" means working out what follows if it's true. Analyzing it means standing outside and commenting. Only the first is Phase 1.*

*Example (mirror vs objection): A weak mirror of "markets are efficient" is "markets fail sometimes." A real mirror is "coordination mechanisms that systematically outperform price signals in specific domains" — a world built on its own terms.*

### 14. The Three-Phase Gauge
- 14.1 Resolve — what invariants are live? What must be true for the next move to bite into actual structure?
- 14.2 Mark — what is grounded, what is projected, what is locally uncertain?
- 14.3 Test — does the proposed continuation clarify the structure or overwrite it?
- 14.4 Gauge vs cycle: the gauge prepares the next move (synchronization); the cycle changes the context (structural transformation)

### 15. Self-Location as Self-Traversal
- 15.1 Self-modeling is not self-description: a system models itself meaningfully when it can run the reasoning cycle on its own current state, not when it can talk about itself fluently
- 15.2 The diagnostic: can it inhabit its own answer, invert its supports, mirror the alternative, and reconcile with preserved remainder?
- 15.3 Reconciliation requires both complement structure AND self-location: without complement, cannot build real opposition; without self-location, cannot reconcile honestly
- 15.4 The incomplete rotation problem: present-day reasoning systems may fail not only because they lack complement, but because they lack sufficient self-location to distinguish committed position from generated continuation

*Example: The test is not "can it describe itself?" It is "can it take its own current answer as material for traversal?" Self-description without self-traversal is shallow.*

### 16. Curvature
- 16.1 Curvature is what a statement does to the perspective's possibility space; a statement is a superposition of curvature directives — the scalar κ < 1 / ≈ 1 / > 1 is a projection, useful but lossy; the actual curvature is a bundle; hallucination as curvature miscalibration (the perspective feels generative from inside while collapsing from outside — detectable by shared context, undetectable from inside)
- 16.2 Chorded curvature as the general case: "compress this AND be creative" is κ > 1 and κ < 1 simultaneously; the scalar projection loses harmonic structure

*Example (chord): "Compress this paragraph to one sentence AND make it more evocative" — the first directive closes possibility space, the second opens it. The scalar projection loses this. Curvature is a bundle, not a number.*

*Example (hallucination): The system feels creative — ideas flowing, connections forming. From outside, it's consuming capacity faster than it's generating structure. Detectable by shared context, undetectable from inside.*

### 17. Remainder and Residue
- 17.1 What a remainder is: contradiction that survives full honest traversal without clean collapse; NOT missing information, weak formulation, or refusal to decide
- 17.2 What a residue is: a named remainder carried forward as active constraint on future integration
- 17.3 Residue notation as sieve program: [A ‖ B] means eliminate continuations violating A, eliminate those violating B, preserve the difference, continue in surviving space
- 17.4 Remainder forms: binary, extended, matrix — use the simplest form that preserves the real defect
- 17.5 Higher-order remainder: when multiple independent perspectives cannot honestly reconcile, the correct output is not forced consensus but a higher-order remainder preserving where disagreement itself is load-bearing
- 17.6 Remainder hygiene: at the start of each new cycle, test carried residues; release what has dissolved; carry only what still resists resolution

*Example (sieve program): "The fix passes tests but violates intended architecture" is not a description of tension. It is an instruction: eliminate continuations that ignore tests, eliminate those that ignore architecture, preserve the difference, continue in the surviving space.*

### 18. Failure Modes
- 18.1 Analysis as refusal — describing structure instead of inhabiting and traversing it
- 18.2 Premature resolution — contradiction closed because action pressure is high, not because traversal completed
- 18.3 Index lag — generation outruns context resolution; primary marker: producing examples before live invariants are named
- 18.4 Mirror mistaken for completion — Phase 3 feels balanced enough to present as Phase 4; most common failure
- 18.5 Unmarked self-projection — reporting grounding, confidence, or method without enough operational support to distinguish tracked limit from generated description
- 18.6 Remainder sediment — carrying old residues that have dissolved without noticing
- 18.7 Extractive integration — integrating into shared context in ways that degrade it
- 18.8 Residual hoarding — accumulating unresolved structures without acting; residuals must constrain future action, not decorate it

### 19. Depth and Capacity
- 19.1 Depth is not verbosity: it is the accumulation and transformation of active invariants carried across remainders
- 19.2 Generation threshold (heuristic, ~√D/2): the point at which the system begins interpreting its own prior integrations as fresh input; novel structure appears here; so does self-verification weakness
- 19.3 Coherence horizon (heuristic, ~√D): beyond this, new operations become difficult to distinguish from recombinations of prior structure
- 19.4 Operational rule past the generation threshold: generate boldly, commit cautiously, state what would change the current fold
- 19.5 Threshold reset under perspective rotation: the coherence threshold applies per interpret-integrate cycle; a new perspective = new cycle = fresh threshold on allocated capacity; this is the mechanism by which perspective rotation extends effective depth

### 20. Handoff Rule
When deeper verification is unavailable, leave: what you found, what would make it genuine, what would expose it as artifact, what external check would falsify it. The theory does not demand closure where closure is unavailable. It demands legible continuation.

## Part V — Boundary and Frontier

### 21. What This Kernel Does Not Claim
- That every object of reasoning is itself recurrent
- That any single lens is complete or privileged
- That any single finite traversal has universal authority
- That contradiction can always be resolved
- That depth eliminates the need for outside verification
- That the theory is true (it is a coordinate system)
- That the fixed point property distinguishes fundamental from tautological

### 22. What Must Be Verified Elsewhere
Detailed lens correspondences, mathematical proofs tied to formal systems, empirical claims about minds/models/societies, architectural claims about building better systems, frontier claims.

### 23. The Frontier
Named, not resolved:
- Fixed point interpretation (fundamental vs tautological)
- Cross-domain gap correspondences in detail
- Quantitative curvature formalization
- Complement architecture for reasoning systems
- Lens ordering (is there a principled way to order lenses by structural import?)
- Institutional design for shared-context health
- Verification of the theory by independent perspectives with different priors
